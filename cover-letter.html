Dear Dr. Adidam and Members of the Search Committee,

I am writing to apply for the Instructor position in Business Statistics (Posting 2026F-00138) in the College of Business Administration at the University of Nebraska at Omaha. The position sits at the intersection of applied statistical practice and teaching — exactly where I have spent the better part of my career — and UNO's metropolitan mission and AACSB-accredited environment are contexts I find genuinely compelling.

My qualifications align directly with what you are seeking. I hold an M.S. in Statistics from Iowa State University and completed doctoral-level coursework in quantitative methods. From 2013 to 2017 I served as a full-time faculty member in the Department of Business Analytics at the University of Colorado Denver, teaching four to seven courses per semester at both the undergraduate and graduate level. The courses I taught there — business statistics, regression and predictive modeling, decision analysis, and analytics computing — map closely onto the Business Statistics sequence that is central to this role. That experience means I can step directly into a four-section-per-semester load, because I have done it.

What I bring beyond formal teaching credentials is a decade of active industry practice applying statistics to real business decisions. I have built pricing algorithms and experiment frameworks at Thumbtack, media mix and attribution models at NerdWallet, demand forecasting and workforce planning models at a billion-dollar healthcare company, and causal inference frameworks for ecommerce product decisions at Intuit Mailchimp. That background is directly relevant to a Business Statistics course whose explicit goal is preparing students for data-informed decision-making in business contexts. When I teach confidence intervals, I can illustrate them with an A/B test I actually ran. When I teach regression, I can connect it to a pricing or forecasting model a student might build in their first job. Keeping curriculum current and making technical methods visibly useful to future business professionals is not a challenge I have to theorize about — it is something I do every day.

I also have substantive experience designing statistics curriculum from the ground up. As Curriculum Lead at Udacity, I directed cross-functional teams to build a Data Scientist Nanodegree in partnership with IBM, Kaggle, and Starbucks, and authored online statistics and machine learning courses that reached tens of thousands of learners globally. That experience sharpened my sense of what makes quantitative instruction actually work at scale: clear learning objectives, examples students find genuinely motivated, and feedback loops that identify and correct confusion quickly.

UNO's identity as Nebraska's only public metropolitan university is meaningful to me. Business statistics is often the first rigorous quantitative course a student encounters in a business program, and many of the students who take it at a metropolitan institution are working adults, first-generation students, or career-changers who need to see the material as useful and achievable — not just technically correct. I have taught those populations throughout my career, and I am good at it.

I would welcome the opportunity to discuss the position further. Thank you for your consideration.

Sincerely,
