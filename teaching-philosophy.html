Joshua Bernhard

Statement of Teaching Philosophy

Statistics is a Tool for Making Sense of Uncertainty

I came to statistics through mathematics, but I stayed because of what statistics does. It is the discipline that teaches us to make rigorous, honest claims in the presence of uncertainty — and that skill is increasingly indispensable across every field a student might enter. My central goal as a teacher is to help students genuinely understand this: not to memorize procedures, but to develop the judgment to apply statistical thinking to the problems they will actually face.

That conviction has been tested and refined over more than a decade of instruction: leading independent undergraduate statistics sections, teaching a full course load of undergraduate and graduate statistics and analytics courses at the collegiate level, designing and delivering an intensive 13-week data science program, and building online statistics curriculum that reached tens of thousands of learners worldwide. Each of those contexts demanded something different from me as a teacher, and that breadth is the foundation of my practice.

Making Difficult Ideas Concrete

The most common barrier in statistics education is not the mathematics — it is the abstraction. Students who encounter a p-value or a confidence interval for the first time often learn a rule without a reason. My teaching addresses this by grounding every concept in a context the student already cares about.

In introductory statistics, I open with questions students find genuinely puzzling: How do we know a drug works if we can only test it on some people? How do polls predict elections from a sample of a few thousand? The machinery of inference follows naturally once the underlying question is alive in the room. I write examples on the board alongside students, pausing after each step to ask whether it makes sense — a practice students consistently identify as useful. Students who came to class, took notes, and engaged with examples did well; those who disengaged struggled. That relationship between active engagement and understanding shaped how I structure every course.

At the graduate level, I teach the same ideas with greater rigor and fewer scaffolds. In courses like Decision Analysis and Predictive Modeling with Big Data, I expect students to build probabilistic and machine learning models from the ground up — making assumptions explicit, checking them, and defending their choices. The real-world industry experience I bring to these courses is not incidental: students at both the undergraduate and graduate level benefit from knowing that the methods we discuss are actively used to make consequential decisions in healthcare, finance, technology, and marketing.

Teaching Across Diversity

I have taught at institutions and in programs serving highly diverse student populations — first-generation students, working adults, students from underrepresented communities, international students, and career-changers. That experience taught me that a rigorous course can also be a welcoming one, and that the two are not in tension.

Students arrive with vastly different mathematical backgrounds and very different relationships with quantitative material. I hold structured office hours every week, not just for those who ask but as a standing invitation. I build courses so that the path to success is clear: show up, engage with examples, and study the problems that appear on assessments. I give feedback that is direct and actionable, and I grade fairly. Across multiple institutions, 70% of students indicated they would take another course from me — a figure I take seriously because it reflects trust built across non-traditional student populations.

Teaching in an intensive bootcamp context pushed me further. My students were career-changers who had left other fields and invested significantly to learn data science. The stakes were high, the backgrounds were varied, and the time was compressed. I learned to diagnose misconceptions quickly, to adjust pacing mid-lecture when the room was lost, and to meet students where they were without lowering what I expected of them. Online teaching added yet another dimension: designing for clarity without the ability to read a room, and building in multiple representations of every concept so that learners with different preparation could find their footing.

Evidence, Feedback, and Continuous Improvement

I am, at heart, an empiricist. I apply the same discipline to my teaching that I apply to data: I look for evidence of what is and is not working, and I revise. I read student evaluations carefully, not defensively. When I notice that a particular topic produces consistent confusion — conditional probability and the interpretation of confidence intervals are perennial examples — I redesign the examples, add a scaffolding exercise, or change the order of presentation. The goal is not a course that stays the same year after year, but one that improves.

My industry background gives me an ongoing source of new material. Every project I work on — whether designing experiments to measure the impact of a product feature, building causal models to understand customer behavior, or developing forecasts for clinical operations — offers a potential classroom example that is timely, motivated, and grounded in real data. Students who understand that statistics is alive and actively used, not a collection of historical techniques, engage differently with the material.

Service and Community

Teaching is not only what happens in the classroom. I am committed to departmental citizenship: attending faculty meetings, contributing to curriculum committees, holding substantive office hours, and supporting students in their academic and early professional development. Throughout my career I have advised students informally on graduate school and careers, hosted public webinars on data science careers, written recommendations, and mentored early-career professionals. Wherever I teach, I aim to contribute as a full member of the department, not merely an instructor passing through.

"Josh is a great guy who really cares about how you're doing both in and out of class. Super understanding and makes a tough subject doable."
— Student evaluation

That is what I am trying to build, every semester: a course that is rigorous, clear, and genuinely human. Statistics is not easy, but it is learnable, and learning it well opens doors. I want my students to walk out of my classroom with both the technical foundation and the confidence to use it.
